Review of “A Comparative Study of Sorting Algorithms: Performance Analysis on Arrays and Linked Lists”

The paper is in the scope of the assignment. It studies sorting algorithms, algorithmic complexity, implementation details, benchmarking methodology, and experimental analysis, which fit well with the scientific paper structure.

This paper presents a comparative study of eight classical sorting algorithms implemented in C and tested on both arrays and linked lists. The author combines theoretical analysis with practical benchmarking and hardware-level profiling using Intel VTune. The paper focuses not only on asymptotic complexity, but also on how memory access patterns, cache behaviour, and branch prediction influence real execution time on modern hardware. The experiments cover multiple input sizes and distributions, which gives the study a broad practical perspective.

The presentation of the paper is clear enough for a fair evaluation of the objectives and achievements. The structure follows the expected academic format, with introduction, formal presentation, implementation, experiments, related work, conclusions, and bibliography. The explanations are generally easy to follow and written in accessible language. The running examples and complexity summaries also help readability.

The main results are sufficiently important for the level of this assignment. The paper successfully shows the practical differences between array-based and linked-list-based sorting implementations and demonstrates how hardware effects influence algorithm performance. The VTune analysis is one of the strongest parts of the paper because it connects theoretical complexity with real processor behaviour. The comparison between Radix Sort, Merge Sort, Quick Sort, and Heap Sort on very large datasets is also useful and relevant.

The results are reasonably non-trivial to achieve. Implementing eight algorithms on two data structures, creating a benchmarking framework, testing multiple input distributions, and integrating Intel VTune profiling require significant work. The experimental setup appears carefully designed, especially the effort to keep measurements fair between algorithms. The discussion about cache misses, branch prediction, and pointer-chasing shows a good understanding of both algorithms and computer architecture.

The results seem to be original in the context of this assignment. While sorting algorithms themselves are classical and well known, the combination of practical benchmarking, linked-list comparison, and VTune hardware analysis on a modern hybrid-core CPU gives the paper its own contribution. The paper also clearly explains which ideas come from existing literature and which parts were independently implemented by the author.

The paper contains enough technical details to understand and mostly verify the correctness of the work. The algorithms, complexity arguments, benchmarking methodology, and hardware analysis are explained in sufficient detail. The correctness proofs are short but acceptable for this level of work. The experimental results also appear coherent and consistent with known theoretical behaviour of the algorithms.

From a technical perspective, the author seems to understand the subject sufficiently well. The complexity discussions are mostly correct, the benchmarking setup is detailed, and the interpretation of the VTune results is reasonable. I have partially checked the technical explanations and did not notice major conceptual errors. However, there are a few small issues that could be improved. Some timing values in Table 2 appear slightly inconsistent for very small inputs, likely because of measurement noise. A few claims could also use slightly more precise wording, especially when discussing CPU metrics and cache behaviour. In addition, some explanations are repeated in multiple sections and could be shortened.

The presentation quality is generally good. The structure is logical, the language is understandable, and the bibliography is properly formatted with explanations for each reference. The related work section is well integrated with the experimental results. The figures and tables are also useful for understanding the conclusions. However, the paper would benefit from a few language corrections and formatting improvements. Some paragraphs are very long and could be split into smaller parts for readability. In a few places the tone becomes slightly too informal for a scientific paper. There are also some minor spacing and typography inconsistencies.

For improvement, I would suggest adding more graphical visualisations of the results, especially charts comparing algorithm growth across input sizes. Some sections of the implementation and benchmarking methodology could also be shortened to reduce repetition. The paper could additionally discuss memory consumption more explicitly, especially for linked-list implementations. It may also help to include a short discussion about compiler optimisations, since all experiments were performed with optimisations disabled. Finally, the conclusions could more clearly separate theoretical observations from experimental observations.

Overall, this is a solid and well-organised paper for a university assignment. The author demonstrates a good understanding of sorting algorithms, benchmarking methodology, and low-level performance analysis. The experimental work is extensive, the discussion is meaningful, and the paper successfully connects theory with practice. While there are some presentation and polishing issues, the work is technically sound and appropriate for the scope of the assignment.

Final recommendation: Accept.
